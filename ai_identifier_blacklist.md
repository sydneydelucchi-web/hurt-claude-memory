## OPENER TELLS
*If a post, email, or caption begins with any of these patterns — rewrite the opener.*

**Rhetorical Question Openers**
- "Have you ever wondered why..."
- "What if I told you..."
- "Why do so many [people / companies / leaders]..."
- "What does [X] actually mean for [Y]?"

**False Intimacy Openers**
- "I've been thinking about this a lot lately."
- "I need to talk about something."
- "Real talk:"
- "Let's be honest."
- "Nobody talks about this, but..."
- "This might be controversial, but..."

**Performative Stance Openers**
- "Hot take:"
- "Unpopular opinion:"
- "Genuine question:"
- "Honest reflection:"
- "A hard truth:"
- "Something I've noticed:"

**Engagement-Bait Openers**
- "X things I wish I knew about [topic]:"
- "Stop doing X. Do this instead."
- "If you're not doing X, you're leaving Y on the table."
- "Here's what nobody tells you about X:"
- "The [X] framework that changed how I [Y]:"

**Announcement Openers (also banned per CLAUDE.md)**
- "I'm excited to share..."
- "I'm thrilled to announce..."
- "We're proud to announce..."
- "I am pleased to announce that..."
- "Big news:"

## STRUCTURAL TELLS
*Formatting and construction patterns that signal AI authorship.*

**Em Dash Overuse**
- Em dashes used as catch-all connectors in place of commas, colons, or parentheses
- Rule: maximum one em dash per piece. If more appear, replace with appropriate punctuation.

**Bullet Overuse**
- Defaulting to bullets for content that reads naturally as prose
- Bullets with perfect grammatical parallelism throughout (every item same structure)
- Numbered lists applied to content that doesn't need sequencing

**Bolding Patterns**
- Unnecessary section headers in short pieces that don't require navigation
- Random bolding mid-sentence without clear emphasis rationale
- Bolding every key term as if creating a glossary
- Unicode bold/italic characters instead of standard formatting (𝗯𝗼𝗹𝗱, 𝘪𝘵𝘢𝘭𝘪𝘤)

**Sentence / Paragraph Patterns**
- The Rule of Three — "adjective, adjective, adjective" or "phrase, phrase, and phrase" used for superficial comprehensiveness
- Uniform paragraph length throughout (no variation for rhythm)
- Uniform sentence length throughout (no short punchy sentences for effect)
- Present participle tail clauses: "...transforming the way we work," "...driving meaningful change," "...reshaping the industry"
- Conclusion or summary that mirrors the introduction almost word-for-word

## TONAL TELLS
*Tone patterns that trained readers identify as machine-produced.*

**Uniformly Positive Framing**
- Everything described as an "opportunity" — including losses, failures, setbacks
- No genuine skepticism, edge, frustration, or ambivalence
- Avoids taking actual positions on anything contested

**Fake Balance**
- "While X has challenges, it also presents opportunities..."
- "On one hand... on the other hand..."
- "It depends on..."
- "There are valid arguments on both sides..."
- Balance that feels performed rather than earned by specific analysis

**Hedging Language (disproportionate frequency)**
- "It's important to note that..."
- "It's worth mentioning..."
- "It should be noted that..."
- "It's worth considering..."
- "Generally speaking..."
- "To some extent..."
- "From a broader perspective..."
- "One might argue..."
- "In many cases..."
- "Arguably,"

**Vague Authority Claims**
- "experts say" without naming them
- "studies show" without citing them
- "many believe" without grounding
- "research indicates" without specifics

**Absence of Texture**
- No personal anecdotes, or anecdotes so generic they could describe anyone
- No specificity of time, place, or people
- No cultural references, no in-jokes, no voice quirks
- Avoids contractions less naturally than humans do

## CLOSER TELLS
*How a piece ends is one of the most reliable AI signals.*

**Engagement-Bait Closers**
- "What do you think?"
- "Thoughts?"
- "Drop a comment below."
- "I'd love to hear your perspective."
- "What's been your experience with this?"
- "Tag someone who needs to hear this."
- "Share this with someone who..."
- "Let me know in the comments."

**Summary Closers**
- "At the end of the day, it all comes down to..."
- "The bottom line is..."
- "In summary, as we've seen..."
- "This is a reminder that..."
- "The takeaway here is simple:"
- "In conclusion,"
- "To summarize,"

**AI Bleed-Through Closers** *(from chat mode, not removed before delivery)*
- "Let me know if you need any modifications."
- "Feel free to adjust this to fit your needs."
- "I hope this helps!"
- "Let me know if you'd like me to expand on any of these points."
- "Happy to revise if needed."

## LINKEDIN-SPECIFIC TELLS

**Structural**
- Every sentence on its own line with blank line between (line-break stacking for artificial drama)
- Emoji used as bullet points at the start of lines (🔑 ✅ 🚀 💡 🎯)
- 🚀 as a closing emoji or section header marker
- The artificially truncated hook ending with "..." to force "see more" click

**Phrase Patterns**
- "In the ever-evolving world of [industry]..."
- "As a [job title], I've learned that..."
- "Here's what [X] years in [industry] taught me:"
- "This is something they don't teach you in school."
- "[Achievement]. Here's how we did it:" *(followed by generic advice)*
- "I used to think X. I was wrong."
- "This is the [framework / system / approach] that changed everything."
- "Follow for more [content type]."

**The Default LinkedIn AI Structure** *(avoid this architecture entirely)*
Contrarian opening → vague personal story framing → numbered list of insights → em dash elaborations on each point → closing rhetorical question

**Overused LinkedIn Intensifiers**
- passionate, grateful, excited, opportunity, impactful, authentic *(especially when used to describe HURT! or the team)*, transformative, thought leader, ecosystem

## EMAIL-SPECIFIC TELLS

**Opening Line Flags**
- "I hope this email finds you well."
- "I trust this email finds you well."
- "I hope this message finds you in good health and high spirits."
- "I wanted to reach out to you today regarding..."
- "I am writing to you today to..."
- "As per our previous conversation..." *(especially when no prior conversation exists)*
- "Dear Sir or Madam,"

**Body Copy Flags**
- moreover / furthermore / additionally as paragraph starters
- Stiff formal register that avoids contractions throughout
- Dense paragraphs where short punchy sentences would serve better
- Excessive qualification mid-sentence ("It may be worth considering that...")

**Closing Line Flags**
- "Please do not hesitate to reach out if you have any questions."
- "Thank you for your time and consideration."
- "Looking forward to connecting and exploring potential synergies."
- "I remain at your disposal."
- "Warm and heartfelt regards,"

**Newly flagged phrases**
- "plays a crucial role in" *(one of the most statistically over-represented phrases in AI output)*
- "it's no secret that" *(throat-clearing authority claim with no grounding)*
- "here's the kicker" *(faux-conversational intimacy tell — AI mimicking casual voice)*
- "aims to" *(as sentence subject — "This piece aims to explore...", "Our approach aims to...")*
- "solid foundation" *(vague competence claim — "built on a solid foundation of...")*

**Rhetorical Micro-Q&A Structure** *(new sub-category — structural tell now spiking)*
- The pattern: short rhetorical question immediately answered in the next sentence
- Examples: "The result? Better outcomes." / "The challenge? Staying consistent." / "The best part? It works."
- This is AI mimicking punchy human voice — but the pattern repeats so uniformly it becomes a tell

**AI Bleed-Through — Extended** *(additions to the existing AI Bleed-Through Closers category)*
- "As of my last knowledge update..." *(model self-disclosure leaking into published copy)*
- "As an AI language model..." / "I'm just an AI..." *(same — remove before any delivery)*
- "I'd be happy to help with that." *(chat-mode response leaking into drafted content)*

**Structural Tell — Circular Restatement** *(new sub-category)*
- AI frequently restates the same core point 2–3 times across a short piece in slightly different language, producing circular reasoning that trained readers identify as filler
- Distinct from the existing "Conclusion or summary that mirrors the introduction" — circular restatement occurs mid-piece and does not require a formal summary structure
